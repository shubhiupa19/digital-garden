A zero-knowledge proof lets you prove you know something without revealing what you know. That sentence sounds like a riddle, but it's one of the most powerful ideas in modern cryptography.

## The Core Idea

Imagine you want to prove to someone that you know the password to a door — but you don't want to tell them the password. ZK proofs make this possible mathematically.

The three properties:
- **Completeness**: If the statement is true, an honest prover can convince the verifier
- **Soundness**: If the statement is false, no cheating prover can convince the verifier
- **Zero-knowledge**: The verifier learns nothing beyond the truth of the statement

## Why This Matters Beyond Crypto

ZK proofs aren't just about blockchain privacy. They're about a fundamental question: **how do we establish trust without requiring full transparency?**

This connects deeply to questions about institutional trust — we've built entire systems (banks, governments, certifications) around the idea that trust requires oversight. ZK proofs suggest an alternative: cryptographic guarantees that don't require anyone to see everything.

## Current Applications

- **zk-SNARKs**: Used in Zcash for private transactions
- **zk-STARKs**: No trusted setup required, quantum-resistant
- **zk-Rollups**: Scaling Ethereum by proving computation off-chain
